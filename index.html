Between late 2024 and the first half of January 2025, I took a pretty long trip to Europe to catch up with some friends. Since most of my friends are doingn their Masters in France, that was my "home base", but since I was going to be there for a while, I wanted to see as many people as possible. This meant making short trips to the UK and Switzerland during my longer stay. Within these countries, I also made quite a few trips to different cities. Armed with my EUrail pass, I was truly unstoppable. It was such a good deal -- even the Paris-London-Paris trip via Eurostar more than made up the price of the 10 day Eurrail pass. Since I anticipated spending lots of time on the train during this trip, I spent quite a bit of time considering which book I would take with me. I would only allow myself one, since I was only bringing one luggage, and would be leaving it in Paris most of the time, bringing only a backpack with me between countries and cities. Finally, I decided on Moby Dick, a book my friend has been bugging me to read for the longest time. A sizably hefty book, good to read over time, and thematically fitting for a journey. I fully expected to be overtaken by brilliant views, and to spend a good chunk of time sleeping. I however, did not expect that for every second spent on rail transit, besides those moments spent in the Eurostar tunnel, I would be completely glued to the window. Not once in my journeys did I even think to bring my book out! (Slightly untrue -- I did think of it, but decided every time that I'd rather look out the window) My favorite moments had to be the early morning train from Paris to London, where passing through Northern Normandy, the fog was so strong out the window, that between glimpses of fields and telephone towers, the view outside was completely white. When the sun started rising behind us, the sun cut through the fog in the most beautiful way, washing the farmland with the most beautiful pastels. Then has to be the journey between Zurich and Interlaken in Switzerland, where the mountains were so tall and stark I physically could not stop myself from going "LOOK!" every three seconds, much to the annoyance of my travelling companions. We also passed by Thun on the way to Interlaken, a fun little jet-lag the game reference, for those in the know.The best part of travelling via rail is that you literally never see the same thing twice. Of course you see similar types of things -- houses, towns, trees, other trains, farms, etc. but even though they may look similar, they are never the same, and always exciting (to me, at least).I found myself pressed up against the window with my digicam and camcorder in hand, trying so desperately to capture the magic of the moving outside. Photographing outside a train window with a digicam is slightly tricky -- depending on how the interior carriage light interacts with the window and outside lighting conditions, it's easy to have flashback in your images. Because the train is going so fast, it's very difficult to snap a clear photo. What I've come to find, after many, many hours of train window photography, is that these elements actually make the photographs so much more special. Like the landscapes, you'll never be able to capture the same photo twice out of a train window. The interior light, exterior weather conditions, cleanliness of the glass, speed of the train, balance of lighting in camera, none of these will ever be the same. In a way, I feel that this makes these photos, blurs, smudges, light leaks and all, all the more special. The smudgy quality that the glass provides gives every train window image a dreamlight state, which is fitting, because these scenes pass by so fast that they really do seem like dreams. I love trains! I wish we had more trains in America. Paris to Zurich is around 373 miles, which is greater than san jose to los angeles, 340 miles. However, unlike san jose to los angeles, where public transit will take you 7 hours and your sanity on a good day, zurich to paris is 4 hours of comfort and great views. I'm honestly writing this at 17h32 the day the website is due so I can't really launch into my transit rant because I'm under a bit of pressure. HOWEVER, I will not be using chatgpt because I am so morally opposed. I had lunch with a friend's sister (coincidentally the same friend with whom i stayed in paris), and she is an "ai entreprenuer". That's cool and all, but she was explaining AI to me, and talking about how it will replace jobs that do not have any "additional societal value". That is genuinely such a bleak way to think, in my opinion. When discussing copyright and infringement or artist rights, specifically in the case of the studio ghibli image generator, she agreed fully that the protections around image are inadequate. However, she insisted that "that's just how things are going now", and thought that if artists were against this theft of their work, they should stop complaining and get into legislative activism for additional protections. I hated that! Anyway, I've written enough now. so that my images can be properly embedded in this lovely text block.

Alexandra
